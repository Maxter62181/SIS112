TrES-2 b

Are you afraid of the dark? Welcome to TrEs-2b, the planet of eternal night. The darkest planet ever discovered orbiting a star, this alien world is less reflective than coal. Inside its atmosphere, you'd be flying blind in the dark. Some scientists think an eerie deep red glow would emanate from its burning atmosphere.

TrES-2 b is an extrasolar planet located approximately 750 light-years away in the constellation Draco, orbiting the star GSC 03549-02811. It was discovered in 2006 by the Trans-Atlantic Exoplanet Survey (TrES). TrES-2 b is classified as a hot Jupiter, meaning it is a gas giant similar in size to Jupiter but with a much closer orbit to its parent star. The planet is notable for being one of the darkest exoplanets ever discovered, reflecting less than 1% of the light that hits it, making it darker than coal.

TrES-2 b orbits its star at a distance of only 5 million kilometers, completing one full orbit in just 2.47 days. Its proximity to the star heats its atmosphere to extreme temperatures of around 980°C (1,800°F). Due to its extreme darkness and heat, the planet may emit a faint red glow, though it remains largely invisible due to its absorption of light.

Scientists believe that the planet's lack of reflective clouds and the presence of light-absorbing chemicals, such as vaporized sodium and potassium, contribute to its unique characteristics. Despite its darkness, TrES-2 b has been observed using the Kepler Space Telescope due to the large dip in brightness it causes when transiting in front of its star.
