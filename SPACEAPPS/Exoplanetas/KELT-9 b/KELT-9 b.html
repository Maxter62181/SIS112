KELT-9b

KELT-9b is so hot that the heat tears molecules apart on the dayside.

KELT-9 b is an exoplanet orbiting the star KELT-9, located about 670 light-years away in the constellation Cygnus. It was discovered in 2017 by the Kilodegree Extremely Little Telescope (KELT) survey. KELT-9 b is classified as an ultra-hot Jupiter, a gas giant with extreme temperatures due to its close proximity to its host star.

KELT-9 b is the hottest known exoplanet, with daytime temperatures reaching up to 4,300°C (7,800°F), which is hotter than some stars. The planet is tidally locked, meaning one side is constantly facing its host star, resulting in a permanent day side and night side. Despite this, even the night side experiences extreme heat.

The planet orbits its parent star every 1.5 days, at a distance of about 0.03 AU (Astronomical Units), which is roughly 30 times closer than Earth is to the Sun. KELT-9, the host star, is an A-type star, much larger and hotter than the Sun, with a surface temperature of around 10,000°C (18,000°F). The intense ultraviolet radiation from the star is causing KELT-9 b's atmosphere to evaporate, slowly stripping away its mass over time.

Due to the extreme temperatures, the atmosphere of KELT-9 b experiences molecular dissociation on its day side, meaning molecules such as hydrogen break apart due to the heat, only to recombine on the cooler night side.

The discovery of KELT-9 b provided astronomers with a new insight into planetary physics under extreme conditions, challenging our understanding of how atmospheres can behave under such high temperatures.
