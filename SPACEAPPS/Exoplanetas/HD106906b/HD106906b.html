HD106906b

HD 106906 b is a fascinating exoplanet, notable for its unusual characteristics and distant orbit around its host star. Discovered in 2013 using direct imaging techniques by the Magellan Telescopes in Chile, this gas giant orbits the star HD 106906, located about 336 light-years away from Earth in the constellation Crux. What makes HD 106906 b particularly intriguing is its massive distance from its star—about 738 AU (astronomical units), which is roughly 20 times farther than Neptune is from the Sun. This wide orbit raises questions about how such a massive planet formed and ended up in such a distant location, as traditional planet formation theories typically expect planets to form closer to their stars.

HD 106906 b is a super-Jupiter, meaning it is much more massive than Jupiter, with an estimated mass of around 11 times that of Jupiter. Its surface temperature is extremely high for a planet, around 2,600 degrees Fahrenheit (1,400 degrees Celsius), because it is still cooling off from its formation. This planet is also relatively young, estimated to be only about 13 million years old, which is very young compared to the 4.5-billion-year-old Earth.

One hypothesis for the planet's unusual position is that it could have formed closer to its star and was later ejected into a distant orbit due to gravitational interactions, either with another planet in the system or with a passing star. Interestingly, the HD 106906 system also has a large debris disk, which may have been shaped by the gravitational influence of HD 106906 b. Researchers have speculated that studying this planet could provide clues about the hypothetical Planet Nine in our own solar system, as the two share similar distant, eccentric orbits.
