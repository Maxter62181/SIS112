51Pegb

51 Pegasi b, often referred to as 51 Peg b or its unofficial name "Bellerophon," is an exoplanet that made history as the first exoplanet discovered orbiting a Sun-like star. Discovered in 1995 by astronomers Michel Mayor and Didier Queloz using the radial velocity method, its detection marked a breakthrough in astronomy, proving that planets existed beyond our solar system. Located approximately 50 light-years away in the constellation Pegasus, 51 Pegasi b orbits its host star, 51 Pegasi, a G-type main-sequence star similar to the Sun.

What makes 51 Pegasi b particularly notable is its status as a "hot Jupiter." Hot Jupiters are gas giants similar in size and composition to Jupiter, but they orbit very close to their parent stars. In the case of 51 Pegasi b, it orbits its star at a distance of just 0.05 AU (astronomical units), much closer than Mercury is to the Sun. This tight orbit causes the planet to complete one revolution around its star in just 4.2 Earth days. As a result, the planet experiences extremely high temperatures, estimated to be over 1,000 degrees Celsius (1,832 degrees Fahrenheit).

Because 51 Pegasi b is a gas giant with no solid surface, it is unlikely to support life as we know it. Its close proximity to its star causes intense stellar radiation, stripping away any potential atmosphere and creating a highly hostile environment. Nevertheless, the discovery of 51 Pegasi b has led to the identification of thousands of exoplanets in the years since, transforming our understanding of planetary systems and the variety of planets that can exist.
