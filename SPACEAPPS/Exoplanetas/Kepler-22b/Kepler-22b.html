Kepler-22b

This artist's conception illustrates Kepler-22b, a planet known to comfortably circle in the habitable zone of a sun-like star. It is the first planet that NASA's Kepler mission has confirmed to orbit in a star's habitable zone - the region around a star where liquid water, a requirement for life on Earth, could persist. The planet is 2.4 times the size of Earth, making it the smallest yet found to orbit in the middle of the habitable zone of a star like our sun. Scientists do not yet know if the planet has a predominantly rocky, gaseous or liquid composition. It's possible that the world would have clouds in its atmosphere, as depicted here in the artist's interpretation.

Kepler-22b is a notable exoplanet discovered by NASA's Kepler Space Telescope in 2009 and confirmed in December 2011. It orbits a star similar to our Sun, located about 600 light-years away in the constellation Cygnus. Kepler-22b is particularly intriguing because it resides within its star's "habitable zone," the region where conditions might allow for liquid water to exist—an essential factor for the potential development of life.

The planet is approximately 2.4 times the size of Earth, classifying it as a "super-Earth." However, little is known about its exact composition. It could be a rocky planet, like Earth, or potentially more gaseous, like Neptune. Its average surface temperature, based on estimates, is around 22°C (72°F), but this depends on the atmospheric conditions, which are still unknown. If Kepler-22b has a thick atmosphere, it could be too hot to sustain life, while a thinner atmosphere might make it more Earth-like.

Kepler-22b's orbit around its parent star takes about 290 days, slightly shorter than Earth's. While scientists have yet to determine whether it has moons or rings, its potential to host life has sparked much interest. It remains one of the key targets for future studies aimed at finding extraterrestrial life and exploring new frontiers in planetary science.
